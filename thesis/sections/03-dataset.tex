\section{The IGC tracklog format}
\label{sec:igcformat}

A flight track is stored as an \emph{IGC file}, the plain-text format defined by the
FAI/IGC Technical Specification for IGC-approved GNSS flight recorders~\cite{fai_igc_spec}.
\rev{Its layout is fixed by that standard, so a fix can be decoded from fixed character
positions rather than parsed heuristically; every downloaded track is validated against the
standard at acquisition (Sec.~\ref{sec:acquisition}).}

Each line is a \emph{record} whose type is set by its first character -- a code assigned by
the standard, not chosen by the logger. The records relevant here are:
\begin{description}
  \item[\texttt{A}] the flight-recorder/manufacturer identifier (first line of the file);
  \item[\texttt{H}] header records (flight date, pilot, glider, geodetic datum, \dots);
  \item[\texttt{I}] the declaration of the optional extra fields appended to every fix;
  \item[\texttt{B}] the GPS fixes -- the body of the file.
\end{description}
A \texttt{B} record stores its fields at fixed character positions. Decoded against the
standard, the example
\begin{center}
\texttt{B\,094329\,4933044N\,00005193E\,A\,00095\,00149}
\end{center}
reads as: UTC time \texttt{09:43:29}; latitude $49^\circ33.044'$\,N and longitude
$000^\circ05.193'$\,E on the WGS84 datum; a fix-validity flag; and \emph{two} altitudes in
metres -- the barometric (pressure) altitude (\SI{95}{\metre}) followed by the GNSS
altitude (\SI{149}{\metre}).

\rev{The validity flag takes two values and concerns the \emph{GNSS} solution only.
\texttt{A} marks a fix whose GNSS altitude is usable; \texttt{V} marks a GNSS drop-out, or a
fix for which the receiver could not resolve altitude, in which case the standard requires
the GNSS-altitude field to be written as zero. A \texttt{V} flag therefore does not make the
fix two-dimensional: the latitude and longitude stay usable and---on a logger with a working
pressure sensor---so does the barometric altitude, which is the channel this analysis adopts
(Sec.~\ref{sec:altchannel}). What a \texttt{V} flag signals lost is the GNSS altitude, not
the horizontal position.}

Decoding a \texttt{B} record from fixed character positions can succeed numerically while
the result is not a valid encoding at all -- a corrupted byte can still parse as a digit.
The parser therefore checks, at decode time and independently of any flight-dynamics
judgement, that the record is actually a legal encoding -- a valid time, valid
latitude/longitude fields, a valid hemisphere letter -- dropping it otherwise, on the same
footing as a record with the wrong length or a non-numeric field \impldetails{impl:igcformat}.
This is a check of \emph{format} validity, distinct from
the \emph{plausibility} checks of fix-level cleaning (Sec.~\ref{sec:fixlevel}, e.g.\ the
inter-fix speed bound), which target fixes that decode validly but would be physically
implausible given the rest of the trajectory.

\flow{The two altitude channels differ in reference and in noise; the analysis adopts the
\emph{barometric} one for the vertical dynamics, for reasons quantified in
Sec.~\ref{sec:altchannel}---a choice that matters because the vertical velocity derived
from it feeds the phase segmentation.} On older
loggers one channel may be absent (recorded as zero).

The sequence of \texttt{B} records is thus a time-stamped three-dimensional trajectory. The
sampling period is typically a few seconds, is not guaranteed to be uniform, and varies
across recording devices -- a point the analysis must handle explicitly.

\section{Glider categories}
\label{sec:glider}

Soaring kinematics depend strongly on the aircraft, so the glider must be identifiable for
any stratified analysis. Three broad categories exist, in increasing order of performance:
\textbf{paragliders} (flexible canopy; slowest, glide ratio $\sim\!8$--$11$, tight turns),
\textbf{hang gliders} (\rev{delta wing, spanning flexible framed wings and rigid wings};
faster, glide ratio $\sim\!10$--$15$), and
\textbf{sailplanes} (rigid aircraft; fastest, glide ratio $\gtrsim\!25$). \rev{They are, in
effect, different aircraft---differing several-fold in cruise speed, glide ratio and turn
radius---which is why the reference study~\cite{vilpellet2026} analyses each separately
rather than pooling them.}

In practice the category is set by the \emph{data source}, since the public databases are
organised by discipline. \flow{Because the FFVL competition is split by discipline
(Sec.~\ref{sec:acquisition}), the present
dataset spans \emph{both} glider types:} the glider-type stratum has two levels, and the
cross-type comparison of the reference study~\cite{vilpellet2026} is therefore already
possible between paragliders and hang gliders. The third category, \emph{sailplanes}, is
collected by different portals and is not yet in hand (Sec.~\ref{sec:acquisition}); until
it is added the cross-type analysis is limited to the two FFVL disciplines.

Within each discipline the flights can be further stratified by \emph{performance class},
recorded in \texttt{aile\_class}; \rev{the two disciplines use different class systems,
collected in Table~\ref{tab:aileclass}. Paragliders follow the EN/AFNOR certification
ladder, hang gliders the FAI/CIVL classes; in both cases the ladder tracks performance
(higher classes fly faster and flatter), so it is the natural within-source stratification
variable, playing the role that glider type plays across sources.}

\section{Data quality and caveats}
\label{sec:dataquality}

A few limitations are already known and will shape the analysis:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Flights without a track.} About \SI{8}{\percent} of paraglider entries and
        about \SI{27}{\percent} of hang-glider entries have no IGC file (older or manually
        declared flights); they carry metadata only. \rev{These are the flights outside the
        \enquote{With GPS} column of Tables~\ref{tab:seasons-para}
        and~\ref{tab:seasons-hang}.}
  \item \textbf{A small residue of unrecoverable files.} A few flights advertise a track
        \rev{whose download fails or returns something that is not valid IGC content}
        (broken or placeholder links); these are recorded as failures and excluded.
  \item \textbf{Heterogeneous logging.} Sampling rate\rev{, availability of the two altitude
        channels, and per-fix positioning noise} depend
        on the device; trajectories must be resampled and quality-filtered before any
        ensemble statistic is computed.
  \item \textbf{Altitude channel.} \flow{A} sizeable minority of loggers record no
        pressure altitude -- \rev{almost a third} of paraglider and roughly a sixth of hang-glider
        flights -- which fall back to the GNSS altitude, \rev{with the channel actually used
        recorded per flight (Sec.~\ref{sec:altchannel})}.
  \item \textbf{Glider metadata.} Flights are paragliders or hang gliders, tagged by source
        (Section~\ref{sec:glider}); the class field \texttt{aile\_class} must be normalised
        before it is used to stratify.
  \item \textbf{Incomplete dates.} Some historical entries have placeholder dates\rev{,
        written as the sentinel value \texttt{0000-00-00} at acquisition. Such flights are
        kept---the trajectory is unaffected---and the sentinel makes them recognizable, so
        any date-based analysis (e.g.\ a per-season stratification) can exclude them
        explicitly.}
\end{itemize}
These caveats motivate the pre-processing described in the remainder of this chapter.

\section{Trajectory pre-processing}
\label{sec:preproc}
Each IGC file is parsed into a sequence of fixes $(t_i,\varphi_i,\lambda_i,h_i)$ -- UTC
time, geodetic latitude and longitude, and altitude -- \rev{with both altitude channels
retained \impldetails{impl:preproc}}. The pipeline then proceeds in a fixed order:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*), itemsep=1pt, topsep=3pt]
  \item choose the altitude channel (Sec.~\ref{sec:altchannel});
  \item clean individual fixes (Sec.~\ref{sec:fixlevel});
  \item trim the ground phases (Sec.~\ref{sec:trimming});
  \item filter out unfit whole flights (Sec.~\ref{sec:flightfilter});
  \item convert to a local Cartesian frame (Sec.~\ref{sec:enu});
  \item enforce a uniform sampling interval within each flight (Sec.~\ref{sec:uniform});
  \item smooth and differentiate the trajectory (Sec.~\ref{sec:savgol}).
\end{enumerate}
The output is a tidy, analysis-ready representation in a growing
\texttt{soaring.analysis} sub-package.

Steps~(i)--(iv) act on the \emph{raw geographic} coordinates, since the only geometric
quantities they need (inter-fix speed and spatial extent) \rev{are scalar distances, which
the great-circle (haversine) formula gives directly from latitude and longitude}, with no
coordinate conversion. The conversion~(v) to the
metric East--North--Up frame (Sec.~\ref{sec:enu}) is then done on the cleaned, trimmed track,
\rev{with the origin at the first fix that survives the trimming, so the tangent-plane
frame is built from good data}\flow{ (Sec.~\ref{sec:enu}).} \rev{Steps~(vi)--(vii) come last for a different
reason: unlike the scalar checks above, they produce \emph{vector} quantities---the
components of the 3D position, velocity and acceleration---which require Cartesian axes to
be expressed in, and
therefore cannot run before the frame of step~(v) exists.}

\subsection{Choice of altitude channel}
\label{sec:altchannel}
\rev{The analysis adopts the \emph{barometric} altitude rather than the GNSS altitude for
the vertical dynamics.} The
reason is one of signal quality in the frequency band that matters. The barometric (pressure)
altitude \rev{resolves altitude changes at the metre level (the IGC format in any case
rounds both channels to whole metres) and carries very little high-frequency noise.}
\rev{Its errors are slow}\flow{:} a constant offset from the ICAO reference, tens of metres up to order
\SI{100}{\metre} \rev{(visible in Fig.~\ref{fig:altnoise}a as the gap between the two
channels; most of that gap is the barometric offset, though the GNSS altitude contributes a
smaller bias of its own)}, and a drift \rev{that already reaches tens of metres within the
half-hour window of Fig.~\ref{fig:altnoise}b, and can grow further over a multi-hour
flight}, following the synoptic pressure and the atmosphere's departure
from the ICAO profile. Slow errors are annihilated by the
differencing that every dynamical quantity involves (a vertical velocity, a displacement increment, an
autocorrelation). The GNSS altitude, by contrast, is \rev{referenced to a fixed geometric
surface (the WGS84 ellipsoid), so it carries no atmospheric offset, but it is vertically
noisier: the satellite geometry resolves the vertical coordinate less well than the
horizontal one (the \emph{vertical dilution of precision}), typically by a factor
$1.5$--$2$, and \emph{multipath}---signals reaching the antenna only after reflecting off
terrain or the pilot's own equipment---adds high-frequency jitter of a few metres.} This is why free-flight variometers derive the
climb rate from the pressure sensor rather than from GNSS.

\paragraph{Empirical confirmation.}
\rev{The data confirm this picture, with one qualification.} Figure~%
\ref{fig:altnoise} compares the two channels over a subsample sized for a robust spectral
estimate (Appendix~\ref{app:psd}) \impldetails{impl:altchannel}. At low frequency the two
agree, tracking the real climbs and glides. The high-frequency behaviour is where they differ,
though not uniformly. For the \emph{typical} flight the two are comparable: both approach
\rev{the same flat high-frequency plateau---the level at which the spectra in panel~c
flatten towards the Nyquist frequency---set by the metre resolution to which the IGC format
rounds \emph{both} channels}, so the median spectra (panel~c, solid lines) nearly coincide.

What separates the channels is the \emph{spread} across flights, the shaded bands. The barometric band stays
tight (that channel is uniformly clean), whereas the GNSS band fans out by one to two
orders of magnitude towards the Nyquist frequency: a substantial minority of flights carry
large GNSS high-frequency noise, from multipath and the vertical dilution of precision, while
most do not. Since the vertical velocity is a finite difference, which amplifies exactly that
band \rev{(differencing scales a spectral component of frequency $f$ by
$2\sin(\pi f\,\Delta t)/\Delta t$, an amplification that grows with $f$: it acts as a
high-pass filter)}, and since one cannot know in advance which flights have poor GNSS reception, the
uniformly clean barometric channel is the safe choice. This
\emph{departs from the reference study}~\cite{vilpellet2026}, whose pipeline derives the
vertical coordinate and velocity from the GNSS altitude; the divergence is deliberate, and one
of the points re-examined here.

Several practical points follow.

\paragraph{\flow{One channel per flight.}}
A trajectory uses a single altitude channel end-to-end;
the barometric and GNSS values are never spliced within a flight, since a
mid-flight switch between two channels offset by tens of metres would inject a
spurious jump.

\paragraph{\flow{Source recorded.}}
\rev{Which channel a flight uses is recorded as a per-flight
attribute of the processed dataset, so the two source groups remain separable in
every downstream analysis; the raw IGC files are never modified
\impldetails{impl:altchannel}.}

\paragraph{\flow{Missing fixes.}}
A flight is used with its barometric channel when it has one, and
the few individual fixes with a missing barometric value are dropped rather than
back-filled from GNSS. \rev{The census quantifies \enquote{few}: only
\StatScanParaBaroMissFlightsPct\,\% of barometric paraglider flights
(\StatScanHangBaroMissFlightsPct\,\% of hang gliders) miss any barometric value at
all, with a median of \StatScanParaBaroMissMedianFixes\
(\StatScanHangBaroMissMedianFixes) missing fixes among them---against $\sim\!10^4$
fixes in a typical track---plus a rare pathological tail. Whether the dropped fixes
are scattered or consecutive, the hole they leave is handled downstream like any
other gap}\flow{ (Sec.~\ref{sec:uniform}).}

\paragraph{\flow{Absent or stuck sensor: the GNSS fallback.}}
A logger with \emph{no} pressure sensor writes the whole
barometric channel as zero (the presence is essentially all-or-nothing, not a
scattering of gaps); such a flight falls back to the GNSS altitude, unfiltered --
a non-negligible minority of flights (Figure~\ref{fig:altnoise}d). Presence alone
is not enough: a stuck sensor writes a constant non-zero value and would feed the
segmentation a vertical velocity of identically zero, so a barometric channel
whose total range over the flight stays below \SI{30}{\metre} is treated as
absent, and the flight falls back to GNSS.
\rev{The fallback rate is measured on the full archive, not estimated from a sample:
\StatScanParaNoBaroPct\,\% of the \num{\StatScanParaTracks} paraglider and
\StatScanHangNoBaroPct\,\% of the \num{\StatScanHangTracks} hang-glider flights scanned
carry no barometric channel \impldetails{impl:altchannel}.}

\paragraph{Mixed altitude sources.}
The mix is acceptable, for two reasons. The \emph{horizontal}
transport analysis, the primary result (MSD and its scaling), does not use the altitude at
all, so every flight is usable there regardless of channel. Altitude enters only the vertical
velocity, hence the segmentation and the phase-resolved observables; there the two source
groups are kept distinct through \rev{the per-flight altitude-source record introduced
above} and their statistics checked for
compatibility (Sec.~\ref{sec:prelim}). If the noisier GNSS-only flights were to bias a
vertical statistic, that analysis is restricted to the barometric flights -- still the large
majority -- while the horizontal analyses keep the full sample. Forcing GNSS on \emph{all}
flights, to avoid the mix, would be the wrong trade: it would needlessly degrade the vertical
signal of the barometric majority to match the worst loggers.

\subsection{Fix-level cleaning}
\label{sec:fixlevel}
\paragraph{Geometry on the raw coordinates.}
\flow{The cleaning runs on the raw geodetic coordinates $(\varphi_i,\lambda_i)$, before
the Cartesian conversion (Sec.~\ref{sec:preproc}), so distances between fixes cannot be
Euclidean;} they are
great-circle (haversine) distances on a
sphere of mean radius $R_\oplus=\SI{6371}{\kilo\metre}$. For two consecutive fixes,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:haversine}
\Delta s_i = 2R_\oplus\arcsin\sqrt{\sin^2\!\frac{\varphi_{i+1}-\varphi_i}{2}
  + \cos\varphi_i\cos\varphi_{i+1}\,\sin^2\!\frac{\lambda_{i+1}-\lambda_i}{2}}\,,
\end{equation}
from which the horizontal speed between the fixes is $v_{xy,i}=\Delta s_i/(t_{i+1}-t_i)$ and,
from the barometric altitude, the vertical speed is $v_{z,i}=(h_{i+1}-h_i)/(t_{i+1}-t_i)$.
\rev{The same distance also yields two whole-flight quantities used below: the \emph{path
length} $L=\sum_i\Delta s_i$, the total distance flown along the track, and the
\emph{extent} $D=\max_i\Delta s(\mathrm{fix}_0,\mathrm{fix}_i)$, the farthest any fix lies
from the first.} \rev{The spherical approximation is ample here. Replacing the WGS84
ellipsoid by a sphere perturbs an inter-fix distance by at most a fraction of the order of
the ellipsoid's flattening, $\lesssim\SI{0.3}{\percent}$---about \SI{3}{\centi\metre} on a
\SI{10}{\metre} step---whereas the horizontal position error of consumer GNSS receivers is
of the order of metres; the correction is therefore far below the noise on the very same
quantity. Working on the raw coordinates also avoids converting them before the trajectory
has been cleaned.}

\paragraph{Design principle.}
Corrupt or physically impossible fixes must be removed without discarding the flight around
them. At the same time, every removed or altered fix modifies the trajectory whose
statistics are about to be measured, and an over-aggressive cleaning biases the result as
much as dirty data would. The cleaning therefore assumes as little as possible about the
motion: only smoothness at the sampling scale, never a model of the transport itself.

The two possible errors have different costs. A spurious fix that survives the tests below
lies within tens of metres of the true track (at most the paraglider speed bound of
Table~\ref{tab:cleaning} times the native step, $\approx\SI{45}{\metre}$ at \SI{1}{\hertz},
and typically within \rev{the absolute floor $\varepsilon_{\min}$ of the
outlier test defined below in Eq.~\eqref{eq:hampel}}), a
displacement the smoothing absorbs; a genuine fix removed from a slow, horizontally
localized phase (a search, a climb) erases a real trapping event, and nothing downstream
can restore it. The cleaning therefore defaults to keeping the data: a fix is removed only
on positive evidence of a defect, and no detector deletes a fix that still carries a usable
horizontal position (at most, a corrupt channel is marked missing and restored at
resampling, Sec.~\ref{sec:uniform}).

\paragraph{Three classes of defect.}
Past the format-validity check made at decode time (Sec.~\ref{sec:igcformat}), which
rejects any record that is not a legal encoding \rev{and is already implemented and
unit-tested}, a surviving fix can still be wrong in one
of three ways, summarized in Table~\ref{tab:cleaning}.

A fix \emph{off the trend} -- a GPS position spike, or a jump out and back within one
step -- is found by a robust local-outlier test, the \emph{Hampel
identifier}~\cite{hampel1974,daviesgather1993}.
\begin{revblock}
Let the window of fix $k$ be the set of
fixes within $\pm w$ of $t_k$, and let
$\tilde\varphi_k=\operatorname{med}\{\varphi_j:|t_j-t_k|\le w\}$ and
$\tilde\lambda_k=\operatorname{med}\{\lambda_j:|t_j-t_k|\le w\}$ be the component-wise
window medians. The residual of fix $k$ is its great-circle distance from that median
point,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:hampelresid}
  r_k=\Delta s\big((\varphi_k,\lambda_k),(\tilde\varphi_k,\tilde\lambda_k)\big),
\end{equation}
with $\Delta s$ as in Eq.~\eqref{eq:haversine}, and the robust local scale of the residuals
is $\operatorname{MAD}_k=\operatorname{med}\{r_j:|t_j-t_k|\le w\}$.
\end{revblock}
The fix is flagged when
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:hampel}
  r_k > \max\!\big(k\,\sigma_k,\ \varepsilon_{\min}\big),
  \qquad \sigma_k = 1.4826\operatorname{MAD}_k,
\end{equation}
Here $\sigma_k$ is the normalized MAD, the median absolute deviation rescaled to estimate
a Gaussian standard deviation~\cite{rousseeuw1993}: the flag requires a departure of more
than $k$ robust standard deviations \emph{and} more than an absolute floor
$\varepsilon_{\min}$ of a few times the GPS noise (working values: $k=5$,
$w=\SI{20}{\second}$, $\varepsilon_{\min}=\SI{15}{\metre}$\rev{, collected in
Table~\ref{tab:fixlevel-params}}; step-by-step definition and
rationale in Sec.~\ref{impl:fixlevel}). A flag alone does not delete: the fix is removed only
when this local flag is \emph{corroborated} by an absolute $v_{xy}$ bound that rules the step
physically impossible\rev{. A genuine but under-sampled tight turn is therefore kept: it may
be flagged by the local test, but it flies at an ordinary speed.}

A fix \emph{on the trend} agrees with its neighbours yet is still wrong as a record -- a
duplicated or backward timestamp, or a run of frozen positions from a lost GNSS lock; its
residual is near zero, so it escapes the outlier test and needs a structural rule instead.

A value \emph{out of range} \rev{concerns the vertical channel: an impossible altitude or
climb rate is caught by an absolute bound that needs no context. (The horizontal analogue,
the $v_{xy}$ bound, never acts alone; it serves as the corroboration of the off-the-trend
test above, Table~\ref{tab:cleaning}.)}

\rev{The thresholds are set in two ways. The absolute bounds are placed on data: the
$v_{xy}$ bounds (\num{45}/\SI{55}{\metre\per\second}, para/hang) and the $|v_z|$ bound
(\SI{13}{\metre\per\second}) sit where the per-fix distributions of
Fig.~\ref{fig:fixlevel} stop decaying, and the altitude window
($[-100,6000]\,\si{\metre}$) is a sensor-plausibility band audited on the same
distributions }\flow{(lower bound negative for the reference-offset reason in the caption
of Table~\ref{tab:cleaning})}\rev{. The remaining parameters---those of the Hampel test
($k$, $w$, $\varepsilon_{\min}$, Eq.~\eqref{eq:hampel}) and of the frozen-lock rule
($\varepsilon$, $\delta_z$, $\tau_{\mathrm{freeze}}$, defined with
Eq.~\eqref{eq:frozenlock} below)---are working values fixed a priori; their reasonableness
is then checked a posteriori on the fraction of fixes each rule removes (paragraph
\emph{Validating the cleaning} below).}

\rev{(Status: the routine
that walks a trajectory applying the three detectors is still to be built.)}

\paragraph{Removal policy.}
A speed bound alone cannot attribute a defect: the speed belongs to the segment
\emph{between} two fixes and does not identify which endpoint is wrong. The local-outlier
test resolves the attribution, because the residual is a property of a single fix; an
isolated spike is removed without touching its neighbours, and a short burst is removed as
one block, the smallest whose removal restores the trend.

\rev{What happens to a defective fix depends on \emph{which} of its values is corrupt, and
the asymmetry follows from a single criterion: keep every genuine horizontal sample, and
never hide a hole in the horizontal record. In both cases the corrupt value itself is
simply discarded---nothing is estimated in its place at this stage; whatever needs
reconstructing is reconstructed once, at the resampling step (Sec.~\ref{sec:uniform}).}

\rev{If the corrupt value is the \emph{altitude}, the fix is kept and only its altitude is
marked missing. The horizontal position and the timestamp are intact, so the fix still
samples the path---exactly the information the transport analysis measures---and
discarding it would throw that genuine sample away. The missing altitude is restored by
interpolation at resampling; the barometric channel is smooth enough that this costs less
than the sensor noise (Sec.~\ref{sec:altchannel}).}

\rev{If the corrupt value is the \emph{horizontal position or the time}, the whole fix is
deleted. One could imagine the symmetric treatment---keep the fix and mark the horizontal
position missing---but that would be dangerous: the resampling step decides whether to bridge a
hole or to split the flight by looking at the \emph{time gap} between surviving fixes.
Ten consecutive kept-but-positionless fixes would look like ten samples of a continuously
recorded stretch; the gap rule would never fire, and the resampler would lay an invented
straight line across an interval that may hold a full thermalling circle. Deleting the
fixes leaves the hole visible, and the gap rule then does its job: a short hole is
bridged, a long one splits the flight (Sec.~\ref{sec:uniform}). The altitude sample lost
with the deleted fix is the cheap part, for the same smoothness reason as above.}

\rev{The IGC validity flag needs no rule of its own here: a \texttt{V} flag certifies a
degraded GNSS altitude (Sec.~\ref{sec:igcformat}), so on a barometric flight the fix is
untouched, and on a GNSS-fallback flight it reduces to the corrupt-altitude case above.}

\paragraph{Frozen-position runs (GNSS lock loss).}
When the receiver loses its GNSS lock, many loggers repeat the last position for tens of
seconds while the clock keeps running. Such a run passes every test above: horizontal speed
zero, altitude in range, valid timestamps. If kept, it enters the analysis as a long
\emph{waiting time} and contaminates the tail of the waiting-time density $\psi(\tau)$,
the distribution of the durations of the slow, localized phases, through which the
anomalous exponent is determined (Ch.~\ref{ch:next-steps}, Appendix~\ref{app:ctrw}). If
the detector is too aggressive, however, it splits tight thermalling climbs, which are
also slow and \emph{horizontally} localized, and destroys real trapping events. The two
errors enter the same tail with the same weight: a kept freeze inserts a false long wait,
a cut climb removes a real one. They differ in what can be repaired downstream: a kept
run with a flat barometric trace is still flagged suspect and swept in the $\psi(\tau)$
sensitivity checks (Sec.~\ref{sec:trimming}), while an erased trapping event is
unrecoverable. A run is therefore cut only when three independent signatures agree,
combined as in Eq.~\eqref{eq:frozenlock} below:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*)]
  \item \emph{Spatial diameter, not step.} A lost lock collapses the whole run into the
        logger's quantisation: the run's \emph{bounding diameter} -- the largest
        great-circle distance between any two of its fixes -- stays below $\varepsilon$,
        \rev{a tolerance of this rule set a few times above the GPS noise (distinct from
        the Hampel floor $\varepsilon_{\min}$, though currently set equal; working values
        in Sec.~\ref{impl:fixlevel}). The run is not a fixed-length window: a candidate
        run is the maximal stretch of consecutive fixes whose bounding diameter stays
        below $\varepsilon$, and the rule then asks how long that stretch lasts.} A
        circling climb instead traces a disc of diameter
        $2R\approx\SIrange{30}{100}{\metre}$, larger than $\varepsilon$ by at least a
        factor of two (quantified below), and clears the test on the diameter alone.
  \item \emph{An independent witness -- the barometric altitude.} A GNSS lock-loss freezes only
        the GNSS position; the pressure sensor is a \emph{separate} instrument. A wing that is
        really thermalling keeps gaining height on the barometric channel, whereas a true freeze
        shows a flat barometric trace. A horizontally motionless run whose barometric altitude
        is still changing at a plausible rate is a (tight) climb, and is \emph{kept}. One
        signature, and only one, may overrule this witness: coordinates repeated
        \emph{byte-identically}, defined in~(iii) \rev{(the structure of
        Eq.~\eqref{eq:frozenlock} makes this explicit: on a barometric flight the witness
        is a flat barometer \emph{or} a byte-identical repeat, so nothing else can cut a
        run whose barometer climbs)}.
  \item \emph{Recorder declarations.} Two signatures declare a defect directly, and they
        carry different weight. A \emph{byte-identical} repeat of the coordinates -- the
        latitude and longitude fields of consecutive \texttt{B} records written character
        for character the same -- is conclusive on its own: no moving receiver rewrites
        the very same bits, so the positions are invented whatever the barometer says. The
        \texttt{V} flag and the zeroed GNSS altitude are weaker: they certify a degraded
        \emph{GNSS altitude}, not a frozen horizontal fix \flow{(Sec.~\ref{sec:igcformat})},
        so they decide
        only where no barometric witness exists, i.e.\ on a GNSS-fallback flight.
\end{enumerate}
A run is classified as a loss of signal only when it is spatially collapsed,
\emph{witnessed}, and sufficiently long,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:frozenlock}
\begin{gathered}
  \underbrace{\operatorname{diam}(\mathrm{run})<\varepsilon}_{\text{(i) collapsed}}
  \;\wedge\;
  \underbrace{W}_{\text{witness (ii)/(iii)}}
  \;\wedge\;
  \underbrace{\Delta t_{\mathrm{run}}\geq\tau_{\mathrm{freeze}}}_{\text{duration}},
  \\[4pt]
  W=
  \begin{cases}
    |\Delta z_{\mathrm{baro}}|<\delta_z \,\vee\, \text{byte-identical} &
      \text{barometric flight,}\\[2pt]
    \texttt{V}/\text{zero-alt} \,\vee\, \text{byte-identical} &
      \text{GNSS-fallback flight.}
  \end{cases}
\end{gathered}
\end{equation}
The run is then marked as a gap and the trajectory is \emph{split} there rather than
interpolated across, so that no motion is invented over an interval whose true path is
unknown; the segments keep their parent's origin and
clock (Sec.~\ref{sec:uniform}).

The witness $W$ is hierarchical. On a barometric
flight no declaration can overrule a climbing barometer; the one
exception is the byte-identical repeat, which is conclusive on its own. Coordinates
repeated bit for bit are invented positions even when the height gain is real, so such a
run is cut even while the barometric altitude climbs, and only the wind drift the receiver
never recorded is lost.

Quantitatively, a real thermalling climb fails the cut on two independent instruments at
once. Geometrically, a circling wing turns at
$R=v^2/(g\tan\phi)$\footnote{\rev{The coordinated-turn relation: in a steady banked
turn the lift $L$ balances gravity vertically, $L\cos\phi=mg$, while its horizontal
component provides the centripetal force, $L\sin\phi=mv^2/R$; dividing the two gives
$\tan\phi=v^2/(gR)$, i.e.\ $R=v^2/(g\tan\phi)$.}}: the tightest paraglider
circles ($v\approx\SI{10}{\metre\per\second}$ at a bank of $\phi\approx35^\circ$\rev{,
ordinary values for tight thermalling}) give
$2R\approx\SI{30}{\metre}$, and hang gliders, which fly faster, turn wider
($2R\gtrsim\SI{50}{\metre}$)\rev{. Against the working value of $\varepsilon$
(\SI{15}{\metre}, Table~\ref{tab:fixlevel-params}), test~(i)
therefore misses by a factor of at least $2$ for the tightest paraglider circles and
$\gtrsim 3$ for hang gliders, more for anything wider---ratios to be rescaled if
$\varepsilon$ moves.} Barometrically, a climb of order \SI{1}{\metre\per\second} sustained
over $\tau_{\mathrm{freeze}}=\SI{60}{\second}$ moves the barometer by
$\gtrsim\SI{60}{\metre}$ against \rev{the flatness tolerance $\delta_z$, which is set well
below that scale (working value in Sec.~\ref{impl:fixlevel})}.

\rev{The duration threshold itself is set on the motion's timescale:} $\tau_{\mathrm{freeze}}$ is
about two thermalling periods and three times the \SI{20}{\second} gap cap of
Sec.~\ref{sec:uniform}, so a run long enough to be cut spans at least two full circles of
any genuine climb, whose diameter test~(i) then sees in full\rev{. Cutting a genuine climb on a barometric
flight therefore requires two failures at once: the geometric margin of test~(i) and the
barometric witness of test~(ii). The only alternative is a byte-identical repeat, which no
moving receiver writes.}

\paragraph{Residual corner cases.}
\rev{The rule assumes a functioning barometric channel, and weakens where that channel is
absent. This is not in tension with adopting the GNSS altitude for the \emph{dynamics} of
fallback flights (Sec.~\ref{sec:altchannel}): there, GNSS is used because it is the only
altitude available; here, the question is different---whether an instrument can
\emph{witness} a suspected GNSS lock loss---and the GNSS altitude cannot, because it comes
from the very receiver whose lock is in doubt and freezes together with the position it
would be checking. On GNSS-fallback flights the witness slot of
Eq.~\eqref{eq:frozenlock} is therefore filled by the recorder's declarations alone (the
\texttt{V} flag or zeroed GNSS altitude, or a byte-identical repeat); the diameter and
duration conditions still apply unchanged, so \enquote{alone} refers only to the witness,
not to the whole rule. The residual error cases are then:}
\begin{itemize}
  \item \rev{\emph{Wrongly cut} (GNSS-fallback only): a circling climb tight enough to
        stay within $\varepsilon$ whose fixes carry a \texttt{V} flag---with no barometer
        to defend it, the declarations cut it.}
  \item \rev{\emph{Wrongly kept} (GNSS-fallback only): an undeclared \emph{jittering}
        freeze. Some receivers, having lost the lock, do not repeat the last position
        byte-for-byte but re-estimate it each second, so consecutive positions differ by a
        metre or two of receiver noise: the run is genuinely frozen, yet neither
        byte-identical nor declared, and it is kept.}
  \item \rev{\emph{Kept, at bounded cost} (barometric flights): a jittered freeze under a
        \emph{climbing} barometer is kept by construction. The cost is bounded because
        the recorded positions all sit within the $\varepsilon$-ball of the last true
        position: what the trajectory loses is only the wind drift the receiver never
        recorded during the run.}
\end{itemize}
\rev{The first two sets are expected to be small---they require a lock loss, a fallback
flight and a borderline geometry at once---but how often each rule fires is measured, not
assumed}\flow{ (paragraph \emph{Validating the cleaning} below)}\rev{. The default to keep
favours preserving real dynamics.}

\paragraph{Completeness of the resampled trajectory.}
The segmentation (Ch.~\ref{ch:next-steps}) needs a defined horizontal position \emph{and}
altitude at every time, since the vertical velocity is its discriminant. Cleaning does not
guarantee this fix by fix -- it may \rev{delete a fix} (a corrupt \rev{horizontal} position) or mark a channel
missing (a barometric spike, a \texttt{V} fix on a GNSS-fallback flight) -- because the
guarantee is established one step later, at resampling (Sec.~\ref{sec:uniform})\rev{. A
\emph{segment} is a maximal stretch of the flight between two splits---equivalently,
between long gaps---analysed as a unit (Sec.~\ref{sec:uniform}).} The invariant is thus
``complete $(x,y,z)$ \emph{within} a segment'', not across a split\flow{, established by
the single audited interpolation of Sec.~\ref{sec:uniform}}.

\paragraph{Flight-level integrity gate.}
Cleaning acts fix by fix, but it also keeps count of how much it had to do. The counters
are taken over the airborne window: defects in a ground phase that trimming removes
anyway must not condemn the flight. If cleaning has removed or reconstructed more than a
small fraction of a flight's airborne fixes, \rev{the flight is dropped whole.}

\paragraph{Validating the cleaning.}
\rev{All the detector parameters are validated by one procedure, run once the cleaning
routine is built (Ch.~\ref{ch:next-steps}):}
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\alph*), itemsep=2pt, topsep=3pt]
  \item \rev{\emph{Freeze the working values.} The thresholds are fixed a priori at the
        values of Table~\ref{tab:cleaning} and Sec.~\ref{impl:fixlevel}; nothing is tuned
        on the transport observables themselves.}
  \item \rev{\emph{Audit the removal fractions.} The cleaning is run over the full
        archive with per-rule counters (the same machinery as the integrity gate): for
        each rule and each discipline, the fraction of fixes flagged and the fraction
        removed. The expectation is that each rule touches only a small fraction of
        fixes; this is the criterion to be verified, not a fact assumed in advance.}
  \item \rev{\emph{Sweep each threshold.} Each parameter is varied around its working
        value and the removal fractions are recomputed. A well-placed threshold sits on a
        plateau: tightening it a little should not change what is removed by much. A rule
        whose removals grow rapidly under a small tightening is biting into real dynamics,
        and its working value is reconsidered. The same sweep covers the split cap of
        Sec.~\ref{sec:uniform}, monitored on its split fractions rather than on removed
        fixes.}
  \item \rev{\emph{False negatives, if needed.} Steps (b)--(c) measure what the cleaning
        removes, not what it misses---the corrupted fixes that pass undetected. If the
        audit leaves a specific rule in doubt, defects of known size (spikes, frozen runs,
        time glitches) are injected into clean tracks and the fraction recovered as a
        function of size measures the false-negative rate directly. This is the expensive
        check, kept as a targeted follow-up rather than run by default.}
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Trimming of ground phases}
\label{sec:trimming}
\paragraph{\rev{Outer ground phases (take-off and landing).}}
The logger starts recording before take-off and stops after landing; these ground phases must
be stripped before any kinematic analysis. Detection uses the \emph{horizontal} speed
$v_{xy}$ alone (from~\eqref{eq:haversine}) -- the vertical speed plays no role here. With
$v_0=\SI{5}{\metre\per\second}$ and $T_0=\SI{30}{\second}$, the retained airborne segment
is $[t_{\mathrm{on}},t_{\mathrm{off}}]$ with
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:trimming}
  t_{\mathrm{on}}=\min\bigl\{t:\ v_{xy}>v_0\ \text{on}\ [t,\,t+T_0]\bigr\},
  \qquad
  t_{\mathrm{off}}=\max\bigl\{t:\ v_{xy}>v_0\ \text{on}\ [t-T_0,\,t]\bigr\}.
\end{equation}
\rev{Take-off is thus the \emph{first} time the speed stays above $v_0$ continuously for
$T_0$, and landing the \emph{last} time it has done so: the same sustained-speed rule read
forward and time-reversed. Reading the landing rule backward from the end matters. A rule
of the form \enquote{cut at the first sustained drop below $v_0$} would fire in the middle
of the flight, because a wing soaring into wind can hold a ground speed near zero for
minutes while genuinely flying.} The persistence $T_0$ prevents a gust or a
GPS glitch on the ground from being mistaken for take-off. A flight with no sustained
stretch at all has no airborne segment and is discarded, with the reason recorded.

\rev{One caveat is structural. The criterion is a \emph{ground} speed, so a slow phase
that happens to sit right at the start or end of the flight---a launch straight into
ridge lift, say---can be clipped along with the ground phase. To keep this failure mode
auditable, the fraction of each flight that trimming removes is recorded per flight, and
its distribution over the archive will be reported as a histogram once the pipeline runs
in full; both $v_0$ and $T_0$ affect that fraction, so they are made explicit
\impldetails{impl:trimming}.}

\paragraph{\rev{Interior ground stints (mid-flight landings).}}
The rule \flow{above} trims only the
\emph{outer} ground phases: a mid-flight landing and relaunch recorded in one log (a
top-landing, say) would survive as an interior near-zero-speed stint and read downstream
as \flow{a false long wait in the tail of $\psi(\tau)$ (Sec.~\ref{sec:fixlevel})}. An
interior-ground detector is therefore
specified, built like the frozen-lock rule: it cuts only on corroborated evidence,
because its dangerous error is the opposite one -- \flow{the soaring-into-wind failure
mode above}. A stint is cut only when both conditions
hold: $v_{xy}<v_0$ \rev{continuously} for at least $T_{\mathrm{ground}}$ (order ten minutes, far
beyond any search), \emph{and} the barometric altitude flat over the whole stint, within
a few metres -- a wing at zero ground speed in real air still moves vertically, the same
independent-sensor argument as Eq.~\eqref{eq:frozenlock}. On a GNSS-fallback flight the
vertical witness uses the noisier GNSS channel with a wider tolerance, and in doubt the
stint is kept.

\rev{A stint that is cut is excised, and the flight is split at the excision: the parts
before and after are treated from then on as separate segments of the same parent flight,
exactly as at a long gap (Sec.~\ref{sec:uniform}). The segment rules apply unchanged---a
phase interrupted by the cut is censored rather than counted as complete, and the
flight-level cuts of Sec.~\ref{sec:flightfilter} are not re-applied to the individual
segments.} Stints shorter than $T_{\mathrm{ground}}$ are not cut but
\emph{flagged}: a wait with sustained $v_{xy}<v_0$ and a flat barometric trace is marked
suspect whatever its length, and the $\psi(\tau)$ fits are re-run with and without the
suspect waits as a sensitivity check -- the same flag-without-deleting pattern as the
outlier identifier. \rev{$T_{\mathrm{ground}}$ and the flatness tolerance are parameters
of this guard; they will join the \texttt{trimming} block of the configuration file when
the guard is implemented, with working values recorded in Sec.~\ref{impl:trimming}.}

\subsection{Flight-level filtering}
\label{sec:flightfilter}
After cleaning and trimming, some flights are still unfit for the ensemble analysis and are
dropped whole. The step is delicate: too aggressive a cut biases the transport statistics -- a
high minimum duration, for instance, preferentially keeps successful long flights and inflates
the apparent persistence of the MSD. The flight-level filtering therefore uses a
\rev{\emph{minimal-threshold strategy}}: for each criterion, the threshold is set to the smallest
value that still clears non-genuine flights (sled runs, tows, aborted or truncated logs), so
the ensemble is disturbed as little as possible. \rev{In practice \enquote{minimal} is read
off the data, not guessed: for each criterion the full-archive distribution of its quantity
is drawn (Fig.~\ref{fig:preproc}, top row), together with the fraction of flights that the
cut alone would retain as the threshold is moved (bottom row). The non-genuine population
shows up as a distinct cluster at the low end of the distribution, separated from the
genuine flights by a sparse tail; the threshold is placed just beyond that cluster, on the
stretch where the retained-fraction curve is flat---there, moving the cut changes almost
nothing, which is precisely the statement that it removes junk and not signal.} Three
track criteria gate a flight, plus a metadata stratifier:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\arabic*)]
  \item \emph{Recorded duration} $T\geq\SI{40}{\minute}$. A cross-country flight spans many
        climb--glide cycles; shorter logs are sled runs or aborted attempts. An upper
        sanity bound $T\leq\SI{16}{\hour}$ removes loggers left running past the flight:
        the census holds \rev{\num{\StatScanParaOverlongCount} paraglider ``flights'' of
        \SIrange{\StatScanParaOverlongMinH}{\StatScanParaOverlongMaxH}{\hour}, with paths
        up to \num{\StatScanParaOverlongMaxPathKm}\,km (and none among the hang gliders),}
        which would poison the long-lag MSD. The cut is
        deliberately \emph{decoupled} from the maximum MSD lag -- the ensemble at lag $t$
        already uses only flights with $T>t$, and the aging in $T$ is diagnosed by stratifying
        in $T$ -- so large-lag analyses restrict to the long-duration stratum rather than
        raising this global cut.
  \item \emph{Flown path length} \rev{$L\geq\SI{20}{\kilo\metre}$}, with $L=\sum_i\Delta s_i$
        from the great-circle steps of~\eqref{eq:haversine}, a floor against degenerate
        logs. \rev{On the census, above \SI{40}{\minute} the cut catches
        \num{\StatScanParaShortPathCount} of \num{\StatScanParaLongEnoughCount} paraglider
        flights (median extent \StatScanParaShortPathMedianExtentKm\,km) and
        \num{\StatScanHangShortPathCount} of \num{\StatScanHangLongEnoughCount} hang-glider

\end{enumerate}

\subsection{From geographic to local Cartesian coordinates}
\label{sec:enu}
The cleaned, trimmed track is next mapped from geographic to local Cartesian coordinates.
\flow{The two remaining steps are different in kind (Sec.~\ref{sec:preproc}):}\rev{
resampling and smoothing operate on coordinates and
their derivatives \emph{componentwise} and therefore need actual Cartesian components to
act on. Latitudes and longitudes cannot play that role---they are angles on a curved
surface, and differencing them componentwise does not yield displacements. The mapping
proceeds in two steps.}

\paragraph{Step 1: geodetic to ECEF.} Each fix is converted to Earth-Centred,
Earth-Fixed (ECEF) coordinates on the WGS84 ellipsoid (semi-major axis
$a=\SI{6378137}{\metre}$, flattening $f=1/298.257223563$, $e^2=f(2-f)$)~\cite{nga_wgs84,esa_navipedia_ellipsoidal_cartesian},
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ecef}
N(\varphi)=\frac{a}{\sqrt{1-e^2\sin^2\varphi}},\qquad
\begin{aligned}
X &= (N+h)\cos\varphi\cos\lambda,\\
Y &= (N+h)\cos\varphi\sin\lambda,\\
Z &= \big(N(1-e^2)+h\big)\sin\varphi,
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
where $N(\varphi)$ is the prime-vertical radius of curvature and $h$ the ellipsoidal height,
measured along the ellipsoid normal\rev{; the transform is derived step by step in
Appendix~\ref{app:geodesy}}.

\paragraph{Geodetic versus geocentric latitude.} \rev{The latitude $\varphi$
in~\eqref{eq:ecef} is the \emph{geodetic} latitude: the angle between the equatorial plane
and the ellipsoid \emph{normal} at the point (Figure~\ref{fig:ellipsoid}). It must not be
confused with the geocentric latitude $\psi$, the angle to the line joining the point to
the Earth's centre $O$. On an ellipsoid the two differ, because the normal does not pass
through $O$: the gap $\varphi-\psi\approx (e^2/2)\sin 2\varphi$ peaks near $45^\circ$ at
$\approx\!0.19^\circ$ (about $11.5'$), which if the two were confused would misplace a fix
by up to ${\sim}\SI{20}{\kilo\metre}$ of ground distance.}

We use the geodetic latitude throughout, and no conversion is needed: WGS84 -- hence the GPS receiver and the IGC
\texttt{B} records (Sec.~\ref{sec:igcformat}) -- reports geodetic latitude, and
\eqref{eq:ecef} with $N(\varphi)$ is precisely the geodetic-to-ECEF transform
that consumes it. \rev{These are also the $(\varphi_0,\lambda_0)$ that orient the local
frame of Step~2 (Figure~\ref{fig:enu-rotation}).}

\paragraph{Step 2: ECEF to local ENU.} Taking the first fix
$(\varphi_0,\lambda_0,h_0)$ \rev{of the \emph{trimmed} track as the origin---the first fix
of free flight, which is close to but not the take-off point on the ground, since the
ground phase has been removed (Sec.~\ref{sec:trimming})}, the ECEF displacement
$\Delta\mathbf{X}=\mathbf{X}-\mathbf{X}_0$ is rotated into the local East--North--Up (ENU)
frame tangent to the ellipsoid at that point,
\begin{equation}
\begin{pmatrix}E\\N\\U\end{pmatrix}=
\begin{pmatrix}
-\sin\lambda_0 & \cos\lambda_0 & 0\\
-\sin\varphi_0\cos\lambda_0 & -\sin\varphi_0\sin\lambda_0 & \cos\varphi_0\\
\cos\varphi_0\cos\lambda_0 & \cos\varphi_0\sin\lambda_0 & \sin\varphi_0
\end{pmatrix}
\Delta\mathbf{X}.
\end{equation}
\rev{with the rotation matrix derived in Appendix~\ref{app:geodesy}. The ENU frame is the
local Cartesian frame in which the whole analysis is carried out: $E$ points east, $N$
north, and $U$ along the local vertical (the ellipsoid normal at the origin). The ECEF
coordinates of Step~1 are only an intermediate representation of the computation---no
observable is ever expressed in them.} \rev{Over the spatial extent of a single flight the
tangent-plane approximation is accurate to well below the GPS noise for the horizontal
coordinates: projecting onto the tangent plane shortens a distance $d$ along the surface
by $\approx d^3/(24R_\oplus^2)$, about \SI{1}{\centi\metre} at $d=\SI{20}{\kilo\metre}$
and \SI{13}{\centi\metre} at \SI{50}{\kilo\metre}, against metre-scale GPS noise
(Appendix~\ref{app:geodesy}).}

\rev{The altitude $h$ that enters this transform is the adopted channel of
Sec.~\ref{sec:altchannel}. The horizontal coordinates are insensitive to that choice, and
the mechanism is visible in Eq.~\eqref{eq:ecef}: $h$ enters the horizontal ECEF components
only through the radius factor $(N+h)$, so switching channel---which changes $h$ by the
inter-reference offset, at most $\sim\!\SI{100}{\metre}$
(Fig.~\ref{fig:altnoise}a)---rescales them by a relative
$\delta h/(N+h)\lesssim 2\times10^{-5}$. Across the full extent of a flight
($\sim$\SI{50}{\kilo\metre}) that is under a metre, below the GPS noise on the same
coordinates.}

\rev{For the vertical, the analysis does not use the rotation's $U$ component as its
working coordinate: it keeps the adopted altitude channel itself
(Sec.~\ref{sec:altchannel}; barometric for most flights, GNSS on the fallback minority),
$z(t)=h(t)$, at its
measured value---the altitude of the first fix is \emph{not} subtracted. Nothing is gained
by re-zeroing: every vertical quantity that matters downstream (the vertical velocity, the
climb/sink discrimination of the segmentation) depends on increments, which are invariant
to the reference, while keeping the measured value preserves the absolute height, an
observable in its own right}\flow{ (potential energy, convective-boundary-layer
structure)}\rev{ (Sec.~\ref{sec:notation}).}

\subsection{Uniform sampling within a flight}
\label{sec:uniform}
The smoothing and differentiation below assume a \emph{uniform} sampling interval, so each
surviving flight's time base is examined next. Loggers record at a fixed nominal rate --
paragliders mostly at \SI{1}{\second} (\StatScanParaDtOneSecPct\,\% of flights), hang
gliders
spread across several rates from \SIrange{1}{10}{\second} with a thin tail beyond
(Fig.~\ref{fig:sampling}) -- but the realised series can carry jitter and
drop-outs. We keep
each flight at its \emph{native} rate\rev{; a common cadence is unnecessary,
since the derivative operator below is given the flight's own $\Delta t$
(Sec.~\ref{sec:savgol}).} What matters is uniformity \emph{within} a flight, not across
flights.

Per flight, let $\Delta t$ be the median inter-fix interval. Three cases arise:
\begin{description}
  \item[Uniform.] All intervals equal $\Delta t$ to tolerance: the series is used as is.
  \item[Mildly irregular.] Isolated jitter or single drop-outs -- no gap beyond the
        split bound $g_{\max}=\min\!\big(10\,\Delta t,\ \max(\SI{20}{\second},\,2\,\Delta
        t)\big)$ \rev{(its two scales are motivated in the paragraph \emph{The split
        bound has two scales} below)} and less
        than \SI{10}{\percent} of the grid missing (the sampling thresholds,
        Sec.~\ref{impl:uniform}): the flight is resampled onto the uniform grid at its
        native $\Delta t$, interpolating across the small gaps only, and the
        interpolated fraction is recorded.
  \item[Broken by a long gap.] A gap longer than $g_{\max}$ -- native, or opened where
        cleaning removed a frozen-lock run (Sec.~\ref{sec:fixlevel})\rev{, or a position
        discontinuity such as a re-acquisition offset, where the track jumps and stays:
        both sides are genuine and only the transition between them is unknown, so it is
        handled exactly like a long gap} -- is not bridged:
        interpolating across it would fabricate dynamics the smoothing could not tell
        from real motion. The flight is \emph{split} at the gap into independently
        analysed segments, each carried forward on its own; what a split means for the
        analysis is stated below.
\end{description}
\rev{The missing-fraction check runs here, and not among the flight-level cuts of
Sec.~\ref{sec:flightfilter}, because it needs the time base and the splits to exist
first. It is evaluated \emph{per segment}, after splitting: a segment whose residual
missing fraction (the grid points that would have to be interpolated) exceeds
\SI{10}{\percent} is dropped alone, and the flight is lost only if no segment survives.
A flight with no long gap is a single segment, so a gap-free flight missing more than
\SI{10}{\percent} of its grid is dropped whole by the same rule.}

\paragraph{The split bound has two scales.}
The relative part, $10\,\Delta t$, tolerates logger hiccups in proportion to the cadence
and caps the number of interpolated grid points at ten. Alone, however, it would let a
slow logger bridge very long holes: \SI{30}{\second} at $\Delta t=\SI{3}{\second}$,
\SI{100}{\second} at $\Delta t=\SI{10}{\second}$. The harm of a bridged gap is set by the
motion's own timescales, not the logger's: an interpolated stretch is a straight line, so
a gap approaching the thermalling period ($\sim\SI{30}{\second}$) could replace a full
circle, and the segmentation downstream would read the invented straight stretch as a
glide. \rev{The absolute cap of \SI{20}{\second} is pinned by three constraints:}
\begin{itemize}[itemsep=2pt, topsep=3pt]
  \item \rev{at least $2\times$ the slowest common cadence (\SI{10}{\second},
        Fig.~\ref{fig:sampling}), so a single missed fix never splits a flight;}
  \item \rev{at most about two thirds of a thermalling period, so a bridged gap can never
        hide a full circle;}
  \item \rev{well below $\tau_{\mathrm{freeze}}=\SI{60}{\second}$, for consistency: an
        excised frozen-lock run of that length is split, so a native gap of comparable
        length cannot be bridged.}
\end{itemize}
One refinement keeps the first constraint true at \emph{every} cadence: the cap
is floored at $2\,\Delta t$, i.e.
$g_{\max}=\min\!\big(10\,\Delta t,\ \max(\SI{20}{\second},\,2\,\Delta t)\big)$, so the
thin tail of very slow loggers (Fig.~\ref{fig:sampling}) also tolerates a single missed
fix; at those cadences a bridged gap inserts at most two grid points. At the dominant
\SI{1}{\second} cadence the relative bound binds ($g_{\max}=\SI{10}{\second}$); from
$\Delta t=\SI{2}{\second}$ to \SI{10}{\second} the \SI{20}{\second} cap does; beyond,
the $2\,\Delta t$ floor.

\rev{On the census, the two-scale bound $g_{\max}$ just defined leaves
\StatScanParaGapSplitCombPct\,\% of paraglider
and \StatScanHangGapSplitCombPct\,\% of hang-glider flights with at least one split; had
the relative part $10\,\Delta t$ acted alone, without the absolute cap, the shares would
be \StatScanParaGapSplitRelPct\,\% and \StatScanHangGapSplitRelPct\,\%. The increase sits
at the slow cadences, where a \SI{20}{\second} hole is only a few missed fixes
(generated macros, Sec.~\ref{impl:uniform}).} \rev{A split is cheap for the ensemble
observables (below) but costly for the \emph{phase} statistics: every split censors the
phase in progress when the gap opens, so splitting too eagerly preferentially removes
long phases from the duration distributions---the same tail the cleaning protects. For
this reason the absolute cap (\SI{20}{\second}) is treated as a working value like the
detector thresholds: it is swept in the validation procedure of Sec.~\ref{sec:fixlevel}
and frozen where the split fractions plateau.}

\rev{The bound is the same for every channel, altitude included. One might expect the
altitude to tolerate wider gaps, since the vertical speed is about an order of magnitude
smaller than the horizontal ($v_z\sim\SI{1}{\metre\per\second}$ against
$v_{xy}\sim\SI{10}{\metre\per\second}$); it does not, for two reasons. First, the
interpolation error over a gap is set by the \emph{curvature} of the signal, not by its
speed: a fast but straight motion is interpolated exactly, a slow but turning one is not,
so a slower channel is not automatically a smoother one. Second, and more decisively, the
altitude feeds $v_z$, the segmentation's discriminant. A long altitude gap interpolated
linearly returns one average slope, which erases any climb--glide transition inside
it---and transitions are exactly where $v_z$ swings fastest: typical glide sink is
$1$--$1.5$\,\si{\metre\per\second} and climb in a working thermal
$2$--$4$\,\si{\metre\per\second} (the envelope visible in Fig.~\ref{fig:fixlevel}b), so a
thermal entry swings $v_z$ by several \si{\metre\per\second} within a couple of seconds,
the worst case for interpolation. A real asymmetry between the channels does exist, but it
concerns \emph{isolated} missing samples, not gap width: a single missing altitude is
reconstructed far more reliably than a single missing position, because the barometric
signal is much smoother---that is what justified \enquote{drop the altitude only} at
cleaning (Sec.~\ref{sec:fixlevel}); it does not justify a wider gap bound for $z$.}

\paragraph{Consequences of a split.}
The observables of Ch.~\ref{ch:next-steps} are affected differently by a split. In
practice a split is bookkeeping rather than a new trajectory: the conversion to ENU
(step~v) runs before this step, so every fix already
carries coordinates and elapsed time referred to the parent \rev{origin}, and the split only
assigns a segment identifier. \rev{\textbf{The key fact is that a segment keeps its
parent's origin and clock} (Sec.~\ref{sec:notation}): a GPS position is absolute, so the
positions after the gap remain exactly as valid as those before it. Ensemble quantities
(the MSD, the propagator) need the horizontal position at a given elapsed time, not the path
travelled in between, so a split costs them nothing---the flight simply contributes no
data at the lags that fall inside the gap.} \rev{Quantities that instead \emph{accumulate
along the path}---the path length, a phase duration, a step length, any time-averaged
window---cannot be evaluated across a stretch whose true course is unknown: sums and
windows for these quantities are always taken inside one segment, never straddling a
gap.} Three cautions follow.
\begin{itemize}[itemsep=2pt, topsep=3pt]
  \item \emph{Censoring at the boundary.} A phase in progress when the gap opens is
        truncated: its duration is a lower bound, not a measurement. \rev{Boundary-truncated
        durations are flagged as censored and simply \emph{excluded}---from the
        $\psi(\tau)$ fits and from the phase-duration histograms alike---rather than
        counted as complete, which would let every split masquerade as a spuriously short
        wait. Censoring-aware estimators are deliberately not used: as long as the
        censored fraction stays small (it is recorded per discipline), plain exclusion
        keeps the estimators transparent at negligible cost, and the recorded fraction
        tells us if that assumption ever fails.}
  \item \emph{A segment is not a fresh flight.} \rev{For observables referenced to the
        time since the flight began---the time-averaged MSD stratified by duration, for
        instance (Ch.~\ref{ch:next-steps})---a segment starting at parent time $t_0>0$ is
        not re-zeroed into a new flight that starts at $t=0$. It is used for what it is:
        an observation window $[t_0,t_1]$ onto a process already $t_0$ old. Re-zeroing
        would mix statistics of young and aged trajectories, which an aging analysis must
        keep apart.}
  \item \emph{No second pass of the flight-level cuts.} \rev{The duration and path cuts
        of Sec.~\ref{sec:flightfilter} were passed by the parent flight and are not
        re-applied to its segments. Re-applying them would be biased in a specific way:
        gaps concentrate in particular conditions (weak reception late in long flights,
        for example), so discarding every sub-\SI{40}{\minute} segment would throw away
        precisely the data recorded around gaps. A segment has to satisfy only what the
        downstream machinery physically requires---enough duration to support the
        smoothing window and the statistics computed within it; one below a minimal
        duration (a configuration key set with the implementation,
        Sec.~\ref{impl:uniform}) is dropped alone, and the rest of the flight is
        retained.}
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Interpolation across short gaps.}
\rev{The scope of this step is set by the split bound: a gap longer than $g_{\max}$ splits
the flight and is never
interpolated, so what is filled here are only the \emph{short} holes, up to $g_{\max}$, that
remain inside a segment. The fill is done per channel, where a channel is one of the three
scalar series on the uniform grid: the horizontal coordinates $E(t)$ and $N(t)$ and the
adopted altitude $z(t)$. The two horizontal coordinates are interpolated independently,
which is the componentwise interpolation of the horizontal position; the barometric and
GNSS altitudes are never mixed here---only the flight's adopted channel is filled
(Sec.~\ref{sec:altchannel}).}

\rev{The horizontal coordinates, which the transport analysis measures directly, are
interpolated with a \emph{monotone piecewise cubic} (a shape-preserving Hermite
interpolant): one cubic per interval, with slopes constrained so that the interpolant
never overshoots the data it passes through. An unconstrained cubic can swing wide of the
sample points, and an overshoot here would invent a position excursion that the smoothing
step would then read as real motion. The altitude is interpolated linearly. The linear
error over a hole of width $g$ is bounded by $\max|\ddot z|\,g^{2}/8$; in the steady
phases where holes are bridged the barometric signal changes slope slowly
(Fig.~\ref{fig:altnoise}), so the error is at the metre scale even at the
\SI{20}{\second} cap and far smaller for typical holes---transitions, where $\ddot z$ is
large, are handled by the flag below.}

\rev{The result is precisely the completeness invariant promised at cleaning
(Sec.~\ref{sec:fixlevel}): \emph{within a retained segment, every grid time carries a
defined $(E,N,z)$}, so the segmentation downstream is never handed a missing value. The
altitude values dropped at cleaning (a barometric spike, a \texttt{V} fix on a
GNSS-fallback flight) are the ones restored here.} \rev{Every filled point carries an
\texttt{interpolated} flag, with a concrete consequence: downstream statistics can
distinguish measured from reconstructed samples, and a phase boundary that the
segmentation places inside a bridged stretch is treated like one at a segment
boundary---the true transition could lie anywhere inside the hole, so the adjoining phase
durations count as censored and are excluded from the duration fits, exactly as at a
split.}

\rev{The gap bound $g_{\max}$ is the criterion called \enquote{sampling regularity} in the
flight-level filtering (Sec.~\ref{sec:flightfilter});}
Figure~\ref{fig:gaps} makes the fraction of flights it leaves whole auditable in the same way
as the duration and path-length cuts \impldetails{impl:uniform}. One caveat carries
downstream: a few observables are intrinsically $\Delta t$-dependent (a finite-difference
velocity, a turning angle)\rev{. The dependence is in the observable's own definition: a
finite-difference velocity is the mean velocity over one sampling step, and a turning
angle is the heading change over one step, so two loggers recording the \emph{same} motion
at \SI{1}{\second} and \SI{10}{\second} produce systematically different distributions of
both. Pooling all flights would blend this instrument artefact into the physics; where
such observables are compared across flights, the ensemble is therefore stratified by rate
or restricted to the dominant one.}

\subsection{Smoothing and differentiation}
\label{sec:savgol}
Velocity and acceleration drive the kinematics and the segmentation, but differencing raw GPS
\rev{coordinate series} amplifies the high-frequency noise characterised in Sec.~\ref{sec:altchannel}. A
\textbf{Savitzky--Golay} filter~\cite{savitzky1964} solves both problems at once: it fits a low-order polynomial
to a sliding window by least squares and reads off that polynomial, or its derivatives, at
the window centre, returning smoothed position, velocity and acceleration
($\mathrm{deriv}=0,1,2$) in one pass per component.\footnote{\rev{Concretely: in the
window of $w$ samples centred on $t_i$, the coefficients of the polynomial
$P(t)=\sum_{k=0}^{p}a_k\,(t-t_i)^k$ minimize $\sum_j\big(x_j-P(t_j)\big)^2$; the smoothed
value is $a_0$, the velocity $a_1$, the acceleration $2a_2$. Because the grid is uniform,
the $a_k$ are fixed linear combinations of the window samples, so the whole filter is a
convolution with precomputed coefficients~\cite{savitzky1964}.}} The derivative outputs are scaled by the
flight's own $\Delta t$ (Sec.~\ref{sec:uniform}), and the window never crosses a segment
boundary. \rev{Near a boundary, where a
centred window no longer fits, the polynomial is fitted on the nearest full window inside
the segment and evaluated off-centre (the standard boundary treatment of the filter); the
first and last few samples of a segment therefore carry a larger variance, and downstream
statistics can exclude them where it matters.} \rev{The filter runs on the resampled grid
of Sec.~\ref{sec:uniform}, so it sees a uniform time base with the short holes already
filled. The filled points carry no independent information---each is a smooth function of
its neighbours---so within a window they mildly correlate the samples; over holes this
short that introduces no bias, and their fraction is small and recorded (heavily
reconstructed flights were dropped, Sec.~\ref{sec:fixlevel}).}

The filter has two hyperparameters, the window length $w$ (odd, in samples) and the
polynomial order $p$, fixed from the measured noise spectrum rather than by eye:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*)]
  \item From the ensemble Welch power spectrum of the raw ENU components on a sample of
        flights (the diagnostic of Sec.~\ref{sec:altchannel} applied to $E$, $N$, $U$), read
        the frequency $f_c$ of the knee where the dynamical signal meets the flat GPS noise
        floor. Its reciprocal is a smoothing timescale $\tau_c=1/f_c$: dynamics slower than
        $\tau_c$ are signal, faster fluctuations are noise.
  \item \rev{Fix $p$ as the lowest order that works. An acceleration requires curvature
        in the fit, so $p\geq2$. One order more is needed for the sake of the
        \emph{velocity}: on a symmetric window, adding an even-order term leaves the
        odd-order coefficients untouched (a parity property of least squares on a
        symmetric grid), so the velocity of a $p=2$ fit is identical to that of a
        straight-line fit; $p=3$ is the lowest order whose velocity estimate responds to
        the local curvature of the track. Going higher only lets the fit follow more
        noise. Hence $p=3$.}
  \item \rev{Set the window per flight from the timescale: $w$ is $\tau_c/\Delta t$
        rounded up to the nearest odd integer (the window must hold an odd number of
        samples so that it has a centre sample to evaluate at), with a floor at $w=5$,
        the smallest window on which a cubic fit is meaningfully determined ($p+1=4$
        coefficients from at least five samples). At the dominant \SI{1}{\second} cadence
        the two rules coincide: $w=5$ samples spans exactly $\tau_c=\SI{5}{\second}$, and
        the smoothing acts at the measured noise scale. At every slower cadence the floor
        is the binding rule (already at $\Delta t=\SI{2}{\second}$, where
        $\tau_c/\Delta t<5$), and the five-sample window then spans \emph{more} than
        $\tau_c$---about \SI{10}{\second} for a \SI{2}{\second} logger, \SI{25}{\second}
        for a \SI{5}{\second} one---so slow loggers are smoothed somewhat more than the
        noise scale requires. The cost is accepted: at those cadences the noise band sits
        at or beyond the Nyquist frequency $1/(2\Delta t)$, so there is little to remove
        at the noise scale in the first place, and these flights are in any case excluded
        from the $\Delta t$-sensitive observables by the rate stratification
        (Sec.~\ref{sec:uniform}).}
  \item Treat the vertical separately from the horizontal, and condition it on the altitude
        \emph{source}. \rev{The a priori expectation was asymmetric: the smooth barometric
        channel would need a \emph{shorter} window than the horizontal coordinates, and
        the noisy GNSS vertical a \emph{longer} one than them. The measurement said
        otherwise: the three knees coincide at
        $f_c\approx\SI{0.2}{\hertz}$ ($\tau_c=\SI{5}{\second}$). The reason is mundane:
        the flat high-frequency floor of every channel comes from the rounding the IGC
        format applies when the file is written---altitudes to whole metres, coordinates
        to thousandths of an arc-minute---rather than from receiver noise, and rounding
        noise flattens the spectrum at the same frequency whatever channel it sits on.
        What distinguishes the noisy GNSS minority is how \emph{high} its floor sits, not
        \emph{where} the knee is (Sec.~\ref{impl:savgol}). So the answer to whether
        $E$, $N$ and $z$ get different smoothing timescales is currently no: the
        per-channel machinery exists, for the noisy GNSS minority, but the adopted
        windows are equal.}
\end{enumerate}
\rev{The choice is then checked on a held-out sample---flights not used to place
$f_c$---with three acceptance criteria: the residual spectrum (raw minus smoothed) must be
flat above $f_c$, showing that what was removed is the noise band and nothing below it;
the acceleration distribution must stay within physical bounds; and the results must be
stable when $w$ is changed by one step} \impldetails{impl:savgol}.

\subsection{Notation}
\label{sec:notation}
Throughout, $\mathbf{r}(t)=\big(x(t),y(t)\big)=\big(E(t),N(t)\big)$ denotes the
\emph{horizontal} position in this local frame and $z(t)$ \rev{the adopted altitude
channel (Sec.~\ref{sec:altchannel}), kept at its measured value (Sec.~\ref{sec:enu})}. By
construction each trajectory starts at the horizontal origin, $\mathbf{r}(0)=\mathbf{0}$, with
the clock reset to $t=0$ at \rev{the start of free flight (the first fix of the trimmed
track, Sec.~\ref{sec:trimming})}, so $t$ is the elapsed flight time (a segment created by
a split at a long gap, Sec.~\ref{sec:uniform}, keeps its parent flight's origin and clock). The vertical is
\emph{not} re-zeroed: \rev{the measured height at the start of free flight} is retained
\flow{(Sec.~\ref{sec:enu})}. The horizontal
velocity is $\mathbf{v}(t)=\dot{\mathbf{r}}(t)$, with magnitude the horizontal speed
$v_{xy}=|\mathbf{v}|$; the vertical velocity is $v_z=\dot z$. The horizontal displacement over
a lag $t$ is $\Delta\mathbf{r}(t)=\mathbf{r}(t_0+t)-\mathbf{r}(t_0)$.
\rev{A terminological rule holds throughout the document: when a statement concerns
$\mathbf{r}$ it says \emph{horizontal position} explicitly, and when it concerns the full
three-dimensional point it says \emph{3D position}; a bare \enquote{position} is avoided.}

\section{Preliminary characterization}
\label{sec:prelim}
\rev{This section concerns the dataset that the analysis will actually consume: the
flights that survive the cleaning, filtering and resampling of Sec.~\ref{sec:preproc}.
Its point is to re-establish the basic statistics on that retained ensemble---which is
why, for instance, the native-rate distribution reappears here: Figs.~\ref{fig:gaps}
and~\ref{fig:sampling} were computed on \emph{every} parsed flight, as a filtering
diagnostic, whereas the analysis needs the same quantities on the flights actually
used.} \rev{The checks below validate basic assumptions and reveal stratification needs;
none of them requires the phase segmentation, so they can run as soon as the pipeline
does, ahead of the transport analysis proper.}

\paragraph{Trajectory statistics.}
\rev{For each retained flight: airborne duration $T$, total horizontal path
length, number of valid fixes and native sampling interval $\Delta t$. Their
distributions over the retained ensemble serve three uses: they describe the dataset the
results will rest on; they expose any outlier that survived the filtering; and, through
$\Delta t$, they fix the per-flight smoothing window (Sec.~\ref{sec:savgol}). On the raw
archive the median recorded duration is \StatScanParaMedianDurH\,h for paragliders and
\StatScanHangMedianDurH\,h for hang gliders, with median flown paths of
\StatScanParaMedianPathKm\ and \StatScanHangMedianPathKm\,km; the same statistics on the
retained ensemble are among the first outputs of the pipeline run.}

\paragraph{Isotropy check.}
\rev{The transport analysis would like to study the one-dimensional marginal of the
propagator in place of the full two-dimensional density (Section~\ref{sec:obs-global}):
a substantial simplification, both statistical and presentational. The reduction is
legitimate only if the process is isotropic, and isotropy cannot be assumed here. The CFD
flights are not races flown together---each is a solo cross-country flight, later entered
into a scoring ladder---but a single flight is still strongly directed: a chosen route,
the orography, the valley winds. Isotropy can therefore hold at best at the level of the
\emph{ensemble}, where take-off sites and route choices vary, never at the level of one
flight; and for the genuinely simultaneous competition flights to be added later
(Ch.~\ref{ch:next-steps}) even the ensemble expectation weakens, since a shared race goal
imposes a common direction. The check is run on the ensemble.}

The primary test is the \textbf{per-component variance ratio}: for an isotropic process
$\langle E(t)^2\rangle = \langle N(t)^2\rangle = \mathrm{MSD}(t)/2$ for all $t$, so the
ratio $\langle E(t)^2\rangle / \langle N(t)^2\rangle$ should be identically $1$. Plotting
it as a function of $t$ reveals both the degree and the scale-dependence of any anisotropy.
A complementary check is a Kolmogorov--Smirnov test comparing the empirical distributions
of $E(t)$ and $N(t)$ at a few selected lags.

If isotropy fails, the two marginals $P(E(t)=x)$ and $P(N(t)=x)$ are analysed
\emph{separately}: this is both the natural fallback and informative in itself, since
unequal scaling exponents $H_E\neq H_N$, or equal exponents with different scaling
functions, signal geometrically anisotropic transport.

\paragraph{Compatibility across strata.}
The dataset spans more than two decades, two glider types (paragliders and hang gliders), and
a range of performance classes (Sec.~\ref{sec:glider}), from recreational EN\,A/B
paraglider wings to CCC racing wings and the flexible/rigid hang-glider classes, whose
kinematics differ substantially. Before treating
\rev{the flights of one discipline} as draws from a single statistical process, i.e.\ before computing ensemble
averages over \rev{a pooled discipline}, the subpopulations must be checked for statistical
compatibility. \rev{The strata of interest are above all the wing classes within each
discipline; the two disciplines themselves are never pooled---every ensemble statistic is
computed within paragliders or within hang gliders}\flow{ (Sec.~\ref{sec:glider}).}

A lightweight way to do this: for each stratum (\rev{a wing class within one discipline,
or the flights of a single season}) compute \rev{the horizontal MSD
$\langle|\Delta\mathbf{r}(t)|^2\rangle$}
as a function of the elapsed time $t$ and overlay the curves on one plot. If they coincide,
the transport statistics are compatible and pooling is justified. If one stratum grows
systematically faster or with a different shape (competition wings, say, cover ground
faster than recreational ones), the strata must be analysed separately, and that difference
is itself a result about the universality of the transport regime.
\rev{Geography is a further stratification axis of the same kind: a map of take-off
coordinates and of displacement end-points at fixed $t$ reveals the clustering imposed by
France's orographic zones (Alps, Pyrenees, Massif Central, plains), and if mountain
flights differ markedly from flatland ones the compatibility check above is repeated
across geographic strata as well.}

\section{Pipeline summary}
\label{sec:pipelinemap}

Figure~\ref{fig:pipelinemap} closes the chapter with the whole processing chain in one
map: every step the data undergo between the raw IGC tracks of Sec.~\ref{sec:igcformat}
and the ensemble handed to the transport analysis, in the order the pipeline applies
them. Table~\ref{tab:pipelinemap} pairs each stage with the level it acts at, its
effect, and the adopted parameters; its last column leads back to the section where
each choice is argued.

Two decisions organise the chain. First the order: every check that runs on the scalar
record as logged --- times, geodetic coordinates, altitude --- comes first
(stages~1--4), and the vector geometry is built only from what survives
(stages~5--7); a coordinate conversion neither repairs a corrupt record nor has to be
undone when one is dropped (Sec.~\ref{sec:preproc}). Second the level: each stage acts
per fix, per flight or per segment, and no stage ever mixes flights --- every trajectory
is cleaned, converted and resampled on its own.

The parameters in the table are the adopted operating point of
\path{configs/preprocessing.yaml}, typeset through macros that
\path{generate_census_stats.py} regenerates from that same file (implementation
details: Table~\ref{tab:impl-repro}), so the summary follows the configuration in
force.
