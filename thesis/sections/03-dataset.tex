\section{The IGC tracklog format}

A flight track is stored as an \emph{IGC file}, the plain-text format defined by the
FAI/IGC Technical Specification for IGC-approved GNSS flight recorders~\cite{fai_igc_spec};
it is the standard output of the recorders used in free-flight competitions. Its layout is
therefore fixed by that standard, not inferred from the files, and the acquisition
validates every downloaded track against it (Chapter~\ref{ch:acquisition}), so conformance
is checked across the whole archive.

Each line is a \emph{record} whose type is set by its first character -- a code assigned by
the standard, not chosen by the logger. The records relevant here are:
\begin{description}
  \item[\texttt{A}] the flight-recorder/manufacturer identifier (first line of the file);
  \item[\texttt{H}] header records (flight date, pilot, glider, geodetic datum, \dots);
  \item[\texttt{I}] the declaration of the optional extra fields appended to every fix;
  \item[\texttt{B}] the GPS fixes -- the body of the file.
\end{description}
A \texttt{B} record stores its fields at fixed character positions. Decoded against the
standard, the example
\begin{center}
\texttt{B\,094329\,4933044N\,00005193E\,A\,00095\,00149}
\end{center}
reads as: UTC time \texttt{09:43:29}; latitude $49^\circ33.044'$\,N and longitude
$000^\circ05.193'$\,E on the WGS84 datum; a fix-validity flag; and \emph{two} altitudes in
metres -- the barometric (pressure) altitude (\SI{95}{\metre}) followed by the GNSS
altitude (\SI{149}{\metre}).

The validity flag is fixed by the standard to two values and certifies the \emph{GNSS
altitude} rather than the horizontal position: \texttt{A} marks a valid three-dimensional
fix (usable GNSS altitude), whereas \texttt{V} marks a two-dimensional fix or a GNSS
drop-out, in which case the standard requires the GNSS-altitude field to be written as
zero. A \texttt{V} fix can thus still carry a usable latitude/longitude and a valid
barometric altitude. The two altitude channels differ in reference and in noise -- the
barometric value is referenced to the ICAO standard atmosphere and is smooth, the GNSS
value is referenced to the WGS84 ellipsoid and is vertically noisier -- and on older
loggers one channel may be absent (recorded as zero). Which altitude to use, and how, is
left open and decided at the analysis stage (Chapter~\ref{ch:next-steps}), because the
vertical velocity derived from it drives the phase segmentation.

The sequence of \texttt{B} records is thus a time-stamped three-dimensional trajectory. The
sampling period is typically a few seconds, is not guaranteed to be uniform, and varies
across recording devices -- a point the analysis must handle explicitly.

\section{Flight metadata and the catalog}

Every flight in the season XML carries a set of metadata as record attributes, which the
acquisition preserves. Beyond the identifiers and links, the fields used downstream are:
the \textbf{date}; the \textbf{pilot} and \textbf{club}; the \textbf{take-off} and
\textbf{landing} sites and the French \textbf{department}; the glider, given as both a model
name (\texttt{aile}) and a class (\texttt{aile\_class}, Section~\ref{sec:glider}); and the
competition quantities -- the \textbf{flight type} (the \emph{scoring} category: free
distance, out-and-return, flat or FAI triangle, hike-and-fly, \dots), the scored
\textbf{distance} and \textbf{points}, and, where present, the \textbf{duration} and average
\textbf{speed}. The flight type is a scoring label and must not be read as a glider or
manoeuvre type.

From these, the pipeline builds a single derived table, the \textbf{catalog}
(\texttt{catalog.csv}), with \emph{one row per flight} across all seasons: the metadata
above plus a \texttt{local\_path} linking each flight to its track on disk and a
\texttt{downloaded} flag. It is the working index of the project -- a flight can be traced
to its file, and back (through the identifier embedded in the file name) to its metadata and
public page, without any external lookup. A smaller \textbf{season index}
(\texttt{seasons\_index.csv}), versioned in the repository, summarises each season by the
number of flights declared, carrying a track, and downloaded; it is the source of the
statistics below. Both tables are regenerated from the archived XMLs on demand
(\texttt{build-catalog}).

\section{Glider categories}
\label{sec:glider}

Soaring kinematics depend strongly on the aircraft, so the glider must be identifiable for
any stratified analysis. Three broad categories exist, in increasing order of performance:
\textbf{paragliders} (flexible canopy; slowest, glide ratio $\sim\!8$--$11$, tight turns),
\textbf{hang gliders} (rigid-framed delta wing; faster, glide ratio $\sim\!10$--$15$), and
\textbf{sailplanes} (rigid aircraft; fastest, glide ratio $\gtrsim\!25$). They differ
several-fold in cruise speed and turn radius -- which is why the reference
study~\cite{vilpellet2026} reports its transport statistics separately for each.

In practice the category is set by the \emph{data source}, since the public databases are
organised by discipline. The FFVL Coupe F\'ed\'erale de Distance is the cross-country
competition for \emph{paragliders}: every flight in the present dataset is a paraglider
flight. Hang gliders and sailplanes are collected by different federations and portals, so
reproducing the reference paper's cross-category comparison requires the additional sources
of Chapter~\ref{ch:acquisition}; within the present data the glider-type stratum is
constant.

Paragliders can still be stratified by \emph{performance class}, recorded in
\texttt{aile\_class}. FFVL labels wings on the EN/AFNOR certification ladder -- EN\,A
(\enquote{A ou 1}, most stable, recreational), EN\,B (\enquote{B ou 1-2}), EN\,C
(\enquote{C ou 2}), EN\,D (\enquote{D ou 2-3}), the \enquote{CIVL Competition Class} (CCC)
racing wings, plus tandem (\enquote{biplace}) and non-certified (\enquote{non
homologu\'ee}) entries. The ladder tracks performance -- higher classes fly faster and
flatter -- so it is the natural within-source stratification variable, playing the role
that glider type plays across sources.

Two caveats bear on any use of \texttt{aile\_class}. First, the field is not clean: in a
minority of rows it contains a wing \emph{model} (e.g.\ \enquote{Enzo 3}, \enquote{Zeno})
rather than a class, so it must be normalised onto the certification ladder -- using the
model name where needed -- before it can serve as a stratum. Second, the class certifies the
wing, not the pilot. Both points are handled at the pre-processing stage
(Chapter~\ref{ch:next-steps}).

\section{Data quality and caveats}

A few limitations are already known and will shape the analysis:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Flights without a track.} About \SI{8}{\percent} of entries have no IGC
        file (older or manually declared flights); they carry metadata only.
  \item \textbf{A small residue of unrecoverable files.} A few flights advertise a track
        that the server does not actually serve as a valid IGC (broken or placeholder
        links); these are recorded as failures and excluded.
  \item \textbf{Heterogeneous logging.} Sampling rate, altitude source, and accuracy
        depend on the device; trajectories must be resampled and quality-filtered before
        any ensemble statistic is computed.
  \item \textbf{Glider metadata.} All flights are paragliders (Section~\ref{sec:glider});
        the class field \texttt{aile\_class} is occasionally filled with a wing model
        instead of a class and must be normalised before it is used to stratify.
  \item \textbf{Incomplete dates.} Some historical entries have placeholder dates (e.g.\
        \texttt{0000-00-00}); these are kept but flagged.
\end{itemize}
These caveats motivate the pre-processing described in Chapter~\ref{ch:next-steps}.
