\chapter{CTRW with power-law waiting times: EA-MSD vs TA-MSD}
\label{app:ctrw}

This appendix derives the ensemble-averaged MSD (EA-MSD) and the time-averaged MSD
(TA-MSD) for the continuous-time random walk (CTRW) with heavy-tailed waiting times, and
shows explicitly that the two differ and that the TA-MSD depends on the observation time
$T$.

\section*{Model}

A walker takes steps of length $l_i$ (iid, symmetric, $\langle l^2\rangle=\sigma^2<\infty$)
separated by waiting times $\tau_i$ (iid) drawn from
\begin{equation}
  \psi(\tau)\sim \frac{\alpha\,\tau_0^\alpha}{\tau^{1+\alpha}}, \qquad \tau\to\infty,
  \quad 0<\alpha<1 .
\end{equation}
The mean waiting time diverges ($\langle\tau\rangle=\infty$), so the walker is anomalously
slow. Let $n(t)$ be the number of steps completed by time $t$, $T_n=\sum_{i=1}^n\tau_i$ the
time of the $n$-th step, and
\begin{equation}
  x(t)=\sum_{i=1}^{n(t)} l_i
\end{equation}
the position at time $t$.

\section*{Ensemble-averaged MSD}

Since steps and waiting times are independent,
\begin{equation}
  \langle x^2(t)\rangle = \sigma^2\,\langle n(t)\rangle .
\end{equation}
For large $t$ the mean number of renewals in $[0,t]$ follows from the renewal equation and
the Tauberian theorem applied to the Laplace transform of $\psi$. Because
$\hat\psi(s)\equiv\int_0^\infty e^{-s\tau}\psi(\tau)\,\mathrm{d}\tau \simeq
1-(\tau_0 s)^\alpha+\cdots$ for $s\to 0$ (a consequence of $\psi$ having a power-law
tail with exponent $1+\alpha$), the renewal theorem gives
\begin{equation}
  \langle n(t)\rangle \sim \frac{t^\alpha}{\tau_0^\alpha\,\Gamma(1+\alpha)}, \qquad t\to\infty .
\end{equation}
Hence the \textbf{EA-MSD} grows sub-linearly,
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{MSD}(t) = \langle x^2(t)\rangle
  \sim \frac{\sigma^2}{\tau_0^\alpha\,\Gamma(1+\alpha)}\,t^\alpha
  \equiv K_\alpha\,t^\alpha , \qquad 0<\alpha<1 .
  \label{eq:ctrw-enmsd}
\end{equation}
The transport is sub-diffusive with Hurst exponent $H=\alpha/2<1/2$.

\section*{Time-averaged MSD and its mean}

For a trajectory of duration $T$, the time-averaged MSD at lag $t\ll T$ is
\begin{equation}
  \overline{\delta^2}(t;T)=\frac{1}{T-t}\int_0^{T-t}
  \bigl[x(t_0+t)-x(t_0)\bigr]^2\,\mathrm{d}t_0 .
\end{equation}
Since $\langle[x(t_0+t)-x(t_0)]^2\rangle=\sigma^2\langle n(t_0+t)-n(t_0)\rangle$, the
mean of the integrand equals $\sigma^2$ times the mean number of steps in any window of
width $t$. The integral
$\int_0^{T-t}[n(t_0+t)-n(t_0)]\,\mathrm{d}t_0$ counts, for each step $k$ that lands at
time $T_k\in[0,T]$, the length of the $t$-wide sliding window that contains $T_k$. For
$T_k$ deep in the interior ($t\le T_k\le T-t$) this length is exactly $t$; the contributions
from the two boundary layers of width $t$ are negligible for $t\ll T$. Therefore
\begin{equation}
  \int_0^{T-t}\bigl[n(t_0+t)-n(t_0)\bigr]\,\mathrm{d}t_0 \approx t\,n(T),
  \qquad t\ll T ,
\end{equation}
and
\begin{equation}
  \overline{\delta^2}(t;T)\approx\frac{\sigma^2\,t\,n(T)}{T} .
  \label{eq:tamsd-approx}
\end{equation}
Taking the ensemble average and using $\langle n(T)\rangle\sim K_\alpha
T^\alpha/\sigma^2$,
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{TAMSD}(t,T)\equiv\langle\overline{\delta^2}(t;T)\rangle
  \approx \sigma^2\,\frac{\langle n(T)\rangle}{T}\,t
  \sim K_\alpha\,T^{\alpha-1}\,t .
  \label{eq:ctrw-tamsd}
\end{equation}
Comparing \eqref{eq:ctrw-enmsd} and \eqref{eq:ctrw-tamsd}:
\begin{itemize}
  \item The EA-MSD scales as $t^\alpha$ (sub-diffusive), whereas the TA-MSD scales as $t^1$
        (linear in the lag) -- a qualitatively different dependence on $t$.
  \item The TA-MSD carries an explicit $T^{\alpha-1}$ prefactor: since $\alpha<1$ this is a
        decreasing function of $T$, so longer trajectories yield a smaller TA-MSD at the
        same lag. This $T$-dependence is the signature of \emph{aging}: the increment
        statistics depend on how long the process has already been observed.
  \item The ratio $\mathrm{TAMSD}(t,T)/\mathrm{MSD}(t)\sim(t/T)^{1-\alpha}\to 0$ as
        $T\to\infty$ at fixed $t$.
\end{itemize}

\section*{Distribution of the TA-MSD: non-self-averaging}

Equation~\eqref{eq:tamsd-approx} shows that $\overline{\delta^2}(t;T)\approx\sigma^2 t\,
n(T)/T$, so the randomness in the TA-MSD comes entirely from $n(T)$, the total number of
steps in $[0,T]$. For a power-law renewal process the rescaled count
\begin{equation}
  \xi\equiv\frac{n(T)}{\langle n(T)\rangle}
\end{equation}
converges in distribution as $T\to\infty$ to a non-degenerate limit law: the
\emph{Mittag-Leffler distribution} $M_\alpha$, whose moments are
$\langle\xi^n\rangle=n!\,\Gamma(1+\alpha)^n/\Gamma(1+n\alpha)$~\cite{he2008}.
This is a broad, non-Gaussian distribution on $(0,\infty)$ that does \emph{not} concentrate
around $\xi=1$ for $\alpha<1$: different trajectories give systematically different values
of $\overline{\delta^2}(t;T)$ even in the limit $T\to\infty$. Hence the TA-MSD is
\emph{non-self-averaging}: the value from a single trajectory is not a reliable estimate
of any ensemble quantity, however long the trajectory.

Quantitatively, the ergodicity-breaking parameter
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{EB}(t,T)\equiv
  \frac{\langle[\overline{\delta^2}(t;T)]^2\rangle-\langle\overline{\delta^2}(t;T)\rangle^2}
       {\langle\overline{\delta^2}(t;T)\rangle^2}
  =\mathrm{Var}(\xi)\;\xrightarrow{\;T\to\infty\;}\;
  \frac{2\,\Gamma(1+\alpha)^2}{\Gamma(1+2\alpha)}-1\;>\;0
\end{equation}
tends to a finite positive constant rather than to zero~\cite{he2008}: the scatter of
$\overline{\delta^2}$ across trajectories survives even in the limit of infinite
observation time (for $\alpha=1$ the constant is $0$ and ergodicity is recovered).
Monitoring the full distribution of $\overline{\delta^2}^{(k)}(t)$ across trajectories,
and not only its mean, therefore diagnoses non-ergodicity directly: the distribution
collapses with growing $T$ for a self-averaging ergodic process and converges to a
non-trivial limit for a non-ergodic one.

For comparison, in an ordinary random walk ($\alpha=1$, finite mean waiting time) the ratio
$n(T)/\langle n(T)\rangle$ tends to $1$ in probability as $T\to\infty$, so the distribution
of the TA-MSD collapses to a delta function, $\overline{\delta^2}(t;T)\to\mathrm{MSD}(t)$
almost surely, and the process is ergodic.
